§ I — INTRODUCTION

A habit that begins before the warning labels do

Smoking and vaping are very common among teenagers. Many young people start because their friends do it, because they are curious, or because vaping feels less harmful than cigarettes. However, nicotine can become addictive very quickly, especially when someone starts young. This is the main issue that The Real Cost campaign focuses on.

The target audience is mainly teenagers who are unaware of the risks of smoking and vaping, teenagers who may start, and teenagers who already smoke or vape. At the same time, the campaign can also be useful for current smokers and non-smokers because it raises general awareness about nicotine addiction.

I chose this campaign because this issue is familiar to me. In Georgia, where I am from, smoking is very common and many people start early. When I was in 10th grade, around 60 out of 75 students were smoking cigarettes or using e-cigarettes. Because of that, I wanted to analyze how this campaign tries to persuade people, and how effective it is.

In a class of 75, sixty already smoked. The risk wasn't theoretical. It was the desk next to mine.

§ II — CAMPAIGN BACKGROUND

An official voice, a youth audience

The Real Cost is a public health campaign created by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) and supported by the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services (HHS). The FDA describes it as a public education campaign focused on preventing young people from using tobacco products ("The Real Cost Campaign"). The official campaign website is the main place where the campaign shares its message.

The campaign focuses on preventing teenagers from smoking cigarettes and using e-cigarettes. Its main message is that smoking and vaping are not harmless habits. They can lead to addiction, health problems, and long-term consequences. The public website also warns that vaping can seriously addict teens and harm the body and brain (The Real Cost).

This campaign is important because many teenagers believe vaping is safer than cigarettes. Even if vaping may seem less serious, most e-cigarettes still contain nicotine, which is addictive. The campaign tries to correct this misunderstanding and show the real risks.

Central Claim
Vaping is marketed as the lighter choice. The Real Cost argues that "lighter" still means addiction.
§ III — CAMPAIGN ANALYSIS

The three appeals, weighed

One of the strongest parts of The Real Cost campaign is logos. The campaign uses facts and research to explain why nicotine is dangerous. It explains how nicotine addiction works and why starting young can lead to long-term dependence. This helps the audience understand the consequences in a logical way.

The campaign also uses ethos effectively. Since it is connected to the FDA and HHS, it feels credible and trustworthy. These are major health organizations, so the message feels official and researched. This is important because people are more likely to believe information when it comes from recognized health authorities.

The campaign also uses pathos, but not as strongly as logos and ethos. It tries to make people feel concerned about addiction and health problems. However, most of the emotional appeal feels more like a warning than a personal story. The campaign explains the danger, but it does not fully show the human side of addiction.

§ IV — CAMPAIGN EVALUATION

Strong on facts. Quieter on feeling.

Overall, The Real Cost is a strong campaign. It clearly explains the dangers of smoking and vaping, and it is supported by trusted health organizations. Its strongest parts are logos and ethos because it gives clear information and feels credible.

However, one weakness of the campaign is that it does not use enough strong emotional appeal. It warns the audience, but it does not include many personal stories or real-life experiences. Because of this, I argue that campaigns about smoking and vaping can become more effective when they use personal stories and emotional examples.

To better understand how students respond to anti-smoking and anti-vaping messages, I created a short survey and shared it with my class. The survey was answered by 45 students. The questions focused on whether students smoke or vape, what kind of anti-smoking messages they find most persuasive, and whether personal stories affect them more than general facts.

§ V — WHAT THE RESEARCH SUPPORTS

Stories, when stakes are high, change behavior

The CDC's Tips From Former Smokers campaign supports this idea. The campaign used real people living with serious health effects from smoking and secondhand smoke. The CDC reports that from 2012 to 2018, the campaign helped more than 16.4 million smokers attempt to quit, and about one million successfully quit because of the campaign ("Tips Impact and Results"). This shows that emotional stories can influence behavior, not just awareness.

Research about health communication also supports this. Shen, Sheer, and Li studied narratives in health messages and found that stories can affect people's attitudes, intentions, and behaviors (105–13). This matters because personal stories can make a health message easier to remember and more persuasive.

Another reason emotional stories matter is that people's decisions are connected to how they understand risk. The Institute of Medicine explains that adolescents who see tobacco as less risky are more likely to start smoking. It also explains that emotional responses can affect health decisions (Scientific Standards). This connects to The Real Cost because the campaign is trying to make teenagers understand the danger before they start.

Because of this, The Real Cost should keep its strong facts and official message, but add more real stories. A story from someone who started vaping casually, became addicted, and later struggled to quit could make the campaign feel more personal. This would make the pathos stronger and help the audience connect to the message more.

§ VI — CONCLUSION

Clear, credible — and one story away from being unforgettable.

The Real Cost is an effective campaign because it gives clear information about smoking and vaping and comes from trusted organizations. It does a good job showing that nicotine addiction is serious.

At the same time, the campaign could be improved by adding more emotional and personal stories. Facts are important, but real experiences can make the message stronger and more relatable. Overall, The Real Cost is clear and persuasive, but it would be stronger if it combined its factual message with more personal examples.
